03 · Research steps

Separate claims before reconciling them

Step 01

Preserve the trigger

Transcribe the LinkedIn ranking and ten project rows into their own provenance file. Do not rewrite the published visual.

Step 02

Capture discussion

Store comment-proposed projects — Wisting, Tangkulo, Bay du Nord, Venus, PAJ — as discussion mentions, not silent inserts into the top ten.

Step 03

Seek primary sources

Match each amount against operator, regulator, and contract disclosures from ExxonMobil, TotalEnergies, Equinor, Petrobras, ANPG, and others.

Step 04

Classify evidence

Label each row verified development CAPEX, identity-only, conflicting scope, or not ranked — keeping currencies and horizons intact.

Step 05

Refuse false precision

Do not convert CAD or NOK, or promote lower-bound estimates, into a new ordinal league table without a common basis.

Step 06

Publish the reconciliation

Keep original, comment, and independent values in separate fields so downstream analysis cannot silently substitute one layer for another.

Not ranked · discussion layer

06 · Takeaway

A ranking is only as honest as its cost boundary

The arithmetic of the ten visual rows explains the US$94bn headline. Arithmetic agreement does not prove a common scope. Traceable industry perspective starts by keeping claims, comments, and primary findings in separate layers — then reconciling in public.

Trigger ≠ dataset

LinkedIn visuals are starting claims, not finished cost tables.

Scope before rank

Development CAPEX and FPSO-only prices must not be mixed silently.

Evidence stays layered

Original, discussion, and independent findings remain separable for audit.

The input was a LinkedIn post. The output was an industry perspective. In under an hour, a simple ranking of the largest FPSO projects became a structured examination of project scope, CAPEX comparability, development status, omitted projects, ownership models, phased versus integrated development, and the strategic assumptions hidden inside the ranking.
We begin with the original LinkedIn visual. Giacomo Prandelli’s post claims the top ten FPSO projects through 2030 are worth about ninety-four billion dollars. Scan the QR code to open the source post. The chart concentrates capital in Guyana, Suriname, Brazil, and one Nigerian project.
A comment under the post asked the decisive question: which investments, besides the FPSOs themselves, are included in these values? That question sets the research agenda. Without a shared cost boundary, the ranking can look exact while comparing unlike scopes.
The method is deliberate. First preserve the trigger visual. Then capture LinkedIn discussion separately. Next consult primary sources. Classify what each source actually proves. Refuse false precision when currencies or horizons do not match. Finally publish a reconciliation that keeps every evidence layer distinct.
Independent sources verify four of the original amounts — Uaru, Whiptail, GranMorgu, and Raia — as full development investments. Petrobras rows are identity-confirmed but amount-open. Bonga conflicts in scope and is flagged. Comment projects remain unranked rather than forced into the table.
The updated ranking keeps the visual’s original order. Green marks independently matched development CAPEX. Grey marks amounts whose scope is still unresolved. Orange marks Bonga’s conflicting newer headline. Five discussion projects sit below as not ranked, with native currencies preserved.
The takeaway is methodological. Treat social rankings as triggers. Establish cost scope before trusting ordinal position. Keep every evidence layer separable so the industry perspective remains auditable as new disclosures arrive.
